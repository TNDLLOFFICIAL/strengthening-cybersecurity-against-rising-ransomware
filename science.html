The Science of Codes: Cryptography

Cryptography: Turning Data into Secrets

At its heart, cybersecurity is based on a branch of mathematics called Cryptography. It is the science of secure communication. Every time you send a text on Snapchat, purchase a game mod, or log into your email, your data travels across the internet as a secret code.

Imagine your data is a piece of text (called Plaintext) that says "HELLO." Before it is sent, your computer uses a math formula called a Cipher to scramble that "Plaintext" into a total mess (called Ciphertext), like "X8#qL." This Ciphertext is what travels across public Wi-Fi networks.

If a hacker intercepts it, they have no idea what "X8#qL" means. They can’t read your message without the specific Mathematical Key that locks and unlocks that code. This whole system is what allows us to have privacy and security online.

1. Symmetric Encryption: The Simple Lock

Symmetric Encryption is like a standard padlock. Both you and the recipient have the exact same digital key. You use the key to lock the message, and they use the exact same key to unlock it.

The problem: This system is very strong, but it has a massive weakness: How do you send the key to the other person without a hacker stealing it? If you send the key over the internet, a hacker can copy it and use it to unlock all your future messages. This is the "Key Exchange Problem."

2. Asymmetric Encryption: The Secure Solution

This is the system that runs the entire internet today. Instead of one key, it uses two different keys that work together: a Public Key and a Private Key.

How it works: A company like Amazon creates a "Public Key" that is shared freely with everyone. Anyone can use that Public Key to *lock* a message (like your credit card info). But Amazon is the only one who has the corresponding "Private Key" that can *unlock* that message.

This completely solves the Key Exchange Problem and makes modern e-commerce possible.
