01. Asymmetric Trapdoor Functions

The science of ransomware is built on the "Trapdoor Function"—a mathematical operation that is easy to perform in one direction but virtually impossible to reverse without a specific piece of information (the private key).

RSA-4096 encryption relies on the Prime Factorization Problem. While multiplying two 2048-bit primes (P and Q) takes microseconds, factoring the resulting 4096-bit number (N) back into P and Q would take a classical computer millions of years.

02. Symmetric AES-256 Saturation

Attackers use a hybrid approach. RSA is too slow for large data. Instead, a unique AES-256 key is generated for every file. AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) uses "Substitution-Permutation Networks" (SPN) to scramble data through 14 rounds of mathematical chaos. The RSA key is then used only to "lock" the tiny AES key.

This hybrid science allows ransomware to lock 10GB of data in seconds, while remaining impossible to decrypt without the master RSA key.

03. Behavioral Cognitive Science

Cybersecurity is not just math; it is the science of human behavior. Attackers leverage "Cognitive Bias" and "Urgency Priming." By overwhelming a human administrator with 500 simultaneous technical alerts (Alert Fatigue), the brain’s executive function fails, leading the human to click "Allow" on a malicious connection just to clear the screen.

04. Post-Quantum Lattice Math

We are currently entering the "Post-Quantum" era. Modern RSA and AES may be vulnerable to Shor's Algorithm running on a stable Quantum Computer. The next generation of defense science uses "Lattice-Based Cryptography," which involves finding the closest vector in a high-dimensional space—a problem that remains unsolved even by quantum processing.
