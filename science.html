Theoretical Foundations

Exploring the cryptographic algorithms and logical pillars of the digital age.

01. Cryptography: The Mathematics of Privacy

At its core, cybersecurity is not a computer science problem; it is a mathematical one. Cryptography is the science of using complex algorithms to transform readable data (plaintext) into an unreadable format (ciphertext). The security of modern civilization depends on the "difficulty" of certain mathematical operations.

The RSA Algorithm & Prime Complexity

RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) is the cornerstone of internet security. It relies on Modular Arithmetic and the Prime Factorization Problem. While multiplying two 500-digit prime numbers is trivial for a computer, reversing that process to find the original primes is a "computationally infeasible" task.

1. Generate two large primes, p and q.
2. Compute n = p * q.
3. Public Key = (e, n) | Private Key = (d, n).
4. Ciphertext (c) = m^e mod n.
5. Only someone with 'd' can solve: m = c^d mod n.

To break a 2048-bit RSA key, a modern supercomputer would need to run for longer than the current age of the universe. This ensures that even if an attacker intercepts your traffic, they see nothing but mathematical noise.

02. Key Exchange: The Diffie-Hellman Protocol

One of the greatest scientific challenges was the "Key Exchange Problem": How do two people share a secret password over an open, public internet without an attacker seeing it? The solution is the Diffie-Hellman protocol.

Think of it as mixing colors. Two people start with a common color. They each add their "secret" color and exchange the mixtures. Because you cannot easily "un-mix" paint to find the original secret colors, an eavesdropper cannot recreate the final combined secret color. In computers, we use Discrete Logarithms instead of paint.

03. Integrity & One-Way Hashing

While encryption protects Confidentiality, Hashing protects Integrity. A cryptographic hash function (like SHA-256) takes an input of any size and turns it into a fixed-length "digital fingerprint."

The Three Laws of Hashing

I. Deterministic

The same input must ALWAYS produce the exact same hash output.

II. Pre-image Resistance

It must be impossible to take a hash and reverse-engineer it to find the original data.

III. Collision Resistance

No two different inputs should ever produce the same hash. If they do, the algorithm is considered "broken."

04. The CIA Triad: The Professional Standard

Professional security engineers view all science through the lens of the CIA Triad. Every defense must address all three:

  • Confidentiality: Preventing unauthorized disclosure (Encryption).
  • Integrity: Preventing unauthorized modification (Hashing/Digital Signatures).
  • Availability: Ensuring reliable access to data (DDoS Mitigation/Redundancy).
